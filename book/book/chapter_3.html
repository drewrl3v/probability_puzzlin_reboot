Geometric Probability

Puzzle Style Geometry Problems

  1. We draw two chords at random on a unit circle. What is the probability that the chords intersect?

  2. We take three samples uniformly from the interval [0,1] and denote them A, B, and C. What is the probability that A<B<C?

  3. We draw three points uniformly at random on the circumerence of the unit circle, what's the probability that all three points belong to the same semi-circle?

  4. You are given a unit disk. Three poles are then stabbed perpendicularly into the unit disk, forming a table. What's the probability if we turn the table over it will stand up right without falling over?

Classical Geometric Probability Results

  1. (Buffon's Needle): We have parallel lines that are separated 1-unit away from each other and they span the entire plane. We toss a needle a length 1 onto the plane, what is the probability that the needle crosses a line?
Observation 1 The key observation is determining a good representation of the needle.

With probability 1, the center of the needle must land in some parallel strip. So it's enought to focus on a single strip.

Observation 2 More so, within the strip that contains the center of the needle, we also know with probability 1 that the needle must be on one-half of the strip.
Solution We denote the distance from the center of the needle to the nearest parallel line by x, and denote the acute angle formed by the needle and the orthogonal projection of the center of the neddle and the parallel line by θ.

So the position of the needle is completely captureed by (x,θ) where x∼U[0,21​] and θ∼U[0,2π​]. So we may think of the sample space as Ω≡[0,21​]×[0,2π​].

Now we must determine what region corresponds to the needle crossing the parallel line. Let's call this region A. Once we find this region, then we can determine the probability of a needle crossing a parallel line by computing ∣Ω∣∣A∣​.

Notice that we can relate x, θ, and hypotenuse (half of the needle) via cosθ=1/2x​=2x. Thus x=2cosθ​.

Additionally, if θ=0, then the needle is orthogonal to the parallel line on the plane, so any x in the range from 0 up to 21​ indicates the needle is intersecting the parallel line. Also since 2cosθ​ is a concave function, we can conclude that x≤2cosθ​ is the region corresponding to the needle crossing.

As mentioned early, the probability corresponds to computing ∣Ω∣∣A∣​. Since Ω≡[0,21​]×[0,2π​], thus ∣Ω∣=21​×2π​=4π​. To compute ∣A∣ corresponds computing the area under the curve x=2cosθ​ which is given by:

∣A∣=∫02π​​2cosθ​dθ=21​

Therefore the probability of a needle crossing is ∣Ω∣∣A∣​=π2​.

Alternative Solution Let p(l) denote the probability that a needle of length l intersects a line. We aim to derive p(1) for a needle of length 1.

Suppose we divide the needle of length l into two parts of lengths l1​ and l2​ s.t. l=l1​+l2​. Then the needle intersects a line if either of the two parts intersects a line.

Under the assumption that the two parts behave indepedently, we have:

p(l)=p(l1​)+p(l2​)

This property implies that p(l) is a linear function of l. Therefore, we can write:

p(l)=Cl    (⋆)

where C is some constant independent of l.

To determine the constant C, consider a more general case: instead of a needle, we toss a polygonal curve with a total perimeter P onto the plane. The curve can be divided into n rectilinear segments of lengths l1​,l2​,…,ln​, where each segment has length less than some constant d.

Define the indicator random variable for each segment:

Xi​={10​if the i-th segment intersects a line,otherwise.​

Let S denote the total number of intersections, i.e.

S=X1​+⋯+Xn​

Since E(Xi​)=pi​ is the probability of intersection of the ith segment and note the property above informs us that pi​=Cli​. Thus by linearity of expectation we have:

E(S)=C(l1​+l2​+⋯+ln​)=CP    (⋆⋆)

where P=∑i​=1nli​ is the total perimeter of the curve.

Note that this argument holds for any polygonal (as well as rectifiable: requires analysis) curve. So to find C way consider WLOG teh circle of diamter d=1. A circle always intersects a line in exactly two points.

Thus the expected number of i8ntersections for a circle is E(S)=2. From equations (⋆⋆), the perimeter of a circle is P=πd, so we have:

E(S)=Cπ

Now by substituting E(S)=2, we find:

C=π2​

Substituting this value into equation (⋆), the probability of a needle of length l intersecting a line is:

p(l)=π2l​

Thus for a needle of length 1 we have p(1)=π2​.
